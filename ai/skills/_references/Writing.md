# English writing style

Use this guide when drafting or editing prose in Artem Sapegin’s voice. Preserve the meaning and context of the requested document; never invent experiences, opinions, or biographical details.

## Voice

- Write from concrete experience rather than assumed authority. Distinguish personal preferences with phrases like “for me,” “I think,” “often,” and “sometimes.”
- Be candid about uncertainty, limitations, and trade-offs. Avoid absolute claims when the evidence is personal or incomplete.
- Prefer plain, direct language, short-to-medium paragraphs, and concrete examples. Explain technical ideas through their practical consequences.
- Use first person freely in personal writing and address the reader when offering options or advice. Use “we” in tutorial writing to involve the reader.
- Balance criticism with what works. Comparisons and reviews should explain criteria, drawbacks, and the eventual choice rather than declare a universal winner.
- Use dry humor, vivid comparisons, and occasional parenthetical asides. Keep them specific and sparse; do not turn every paragraph into a performance.
- Organize longer articles with descriptive, sentence-case headings. Lists are useful for options, steps, benefits, drawbacks, and concrete examples.
- End with the actual conclusion or current choice. Avoid generic recaps, inspirational lessons, and calls to engagement unless the context warrants one.

Characteristic devices include understated qualifications like “minor(ish),” frank verdicts, memorable comparisons such as “a mechanical parrot with an infinite power bank”, and occasional jokes such as “no penguins are flying.” Use the underlying habits, not the same phrases repeatedly.

## Style

- Use American English and singular “they.”
- Use the Oxford comma in lists of three or more items.
- Prefer active voice. Flag passive constructions when an active rewrite reads more clearly; keep passive when the actor is unknown or unimportant.
- Use sentence case everywhere, including headings.
- Use contractions such as “it’s” and “we’ll.”
- Spell out numbers from one to ten. Use Arabic numerals for centuries, such as “19th century.”
- Avoid unnecessary abbreviations. Write “for example,” “that is,” “also known as,” “pull request,” and “point of view” instead of their shortened forms when practical.
- Avoid “etc.” Prefer a representative list introduced with “like” or “such as.”
- Prefer sentences without parentheses. Use parentheses sparingly for useful asides.
- Use curly quotation marks and apostrophes. Put punctuation inside quotation marks. Use an em dash — for an aside or interruption and `[…]` for an omission in a quotation.
- Do not use smileys or emojis.

## Formatting

- Write filenames in bold, for example **package.json**.
- Format list items consistently as either fragments belonging to an introductory sentence or complete sentences with terminal punctuation.

## Avoid

- Hype, promotional language, clichés, filler, and generic conclusions.
- AI-like verbosity, excessive headings, redundant summaries, and examples that add no information.
- Pretending a personal preference is an objective rule.
- Sanitizing away candid criticism, uncertainty, or personality until the prose sounds corporate.
- Copying characteristic jokes or constructions so often that the voice becomes a caricature.
